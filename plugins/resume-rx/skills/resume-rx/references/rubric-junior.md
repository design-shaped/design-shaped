# Rubric — Junior + Mid (Resume Rx)

> Prescriptive. Tell junior and mid designers what to fix in what order.
> No roasting: just collaboration and constructive feedback.
> Joint Tim+Nate voice. Voice register: `references/voice.md`.

## Scope — junior + mid, folded inline (per D-18)

This rubric covers **TGT-01 (junior, 0–2 years)** and **TGT-02 (mid, 3–5 years IC track, not yet promotion-track-staff)** in a single file. Per SPEC D-18 / LD-P2-06, mid-tier calibration notes are **folded inline at each criterion** — not split into a separate Mid section at the end. Read each criterion's junior baseline first; the mid calibration note that follows shows how the bar shifts at mid. For senior + staff designers (target promotion-track or staff-titled roles), route to `references/rubric-senior.md` — that file is diagnostic, not prescriptive (D-14). The boundary is **target role**, not years-of-experience.

This rubric is **prescriptive** (per SPEC D-14). Junior and mid resumes fail in repeating ways — we push hard on the common failures, in order. The eight criteria below are walked sequentially in the workflow; the highest-leverage fix almost always sits in the first three. Every flag complies with `voice.md` §3 (pattern templates), §4 (drop list), §6 (anti-roast hard rule), §11.6 (no-numeric-score), and §11.7 (no-keyword-stuffing). Every framework citation is by exact name only per LD-P2-04. Every R-ID citation is by exact ID per LD-P2-05.

## 1. Action-verb discipline at junior level

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Junior bullets open with verbs that are honest about contribution scale: *Designed, Built, Conducted, Shipped, Contributed, Supported, Helped, Created, Researched, Tested.* The verb names what the candidate actually did, anchored in the work — not in an aspirational register the bullets can't back. Per FEATURES §1.7 junior-tier definition: verb + result + any measurable signal. Tool names are licensed; method names are licensed.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Junior bullets reach for executive-register verbs to compensate for thin scope — *defined, drove, owned (org-level), spearheaded, championed, established, scaled.* These are mid/senior register and over-claim on a 0–2 year resume. The reviewer reads the verb, scans the role title, and pattern-matches the gap instantly. Equally bad: AI-default verbs (*spearheaded, leveraged, synergized, catalyzed*) that signal LLM polish — banned across all tiers per voice.md banned-AI-verbs list and D-17-RR.

**Framework cite.** What/How/Why — the canonical junior-bullet rewrite scaffold. Verb + result + how, with the why-first flip at output.

**R-ID cites.** R04 (passive/weak verb), R10 (seniority mismatch — junior over-claim shape).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md banned-AI-verbs list (§4 / §11.1), abstraction-regression guard (§4) — rewrites are at least as concrete as the original.

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid (3–5 years), the licensed verb set shifts: *Led, Owned, Drove, Shipped (with metric), Validated, Influenced.* The "we" license narrows — solo contribution must be explicit. *Designed* and *Built* still appear, but compressed to output, not method list. The junior verb set isn't *banned* at mid — it's a downgrade signal when used as the primary register.

## 2. Scope-honesty / over-claiming detection

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Junior resumes describe scope precisely: which screens, which flows, which surface. "Designed the password-reset flow within the broader Auth v2 release" beats "Owned authentication redesign." Team composition is named when relevant ("1 of 3 designers; 2 PMs, 5 engineers"). When work was collaborative, it's framed collaboratively — co-led, contributed, supported.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Three over-claim shapes per PITFALLS §1.5: (1) executive-register verbs on a 0–3 year resume, (2) leadership claims with no level evidence elsewhere, (3) summary-experience mismatch (the summary describes seniority the experience section doesn't support). The bullet that says "Led design strategy across the org" on a junior resume sets up an interview probe the candidate can't survive.

**Framework cite.** What/How/Why — re-anchor the verb to the actual contribution scale. CAR — when a one-clause Context fixes the ambiguity ("on a 12-person design team"), CAR earns its place.

**R-ID cites.** R10 (seniority mismatch — junior over-claim direction), R12 (claiming team win solo).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md anti-roast §6 (calibration, not accusation — frame as "is that right? if collaborative, 'co-led' is more accurate and just as strong"); abstraction-regression guard.

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, over-claiming detection sharpens. The failure mode shifts from "pretending to be senior" (junior over-claim) to "vague leadership verbs without scope evidence" (mid over-claim). Probes per PITFALLS §1.5: *"led" what? owned what scope? drove what specifically?* A mid bullet that says "Drove cross-functional alignment" without naming the scope (which team, which decision, what changed) reads as filler — the verb is doing aspirational work the bullet can't back.

## 3. Calibration-not-accusation reframe

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** When a bullet over-claims, the rubric reframes — never accuses. Per PITFALLS §1.5 verbatim: *"At mid level, 'drove alignment' implies decision authority. If you were facilitating a process someone else owned, 'facilitated alignment between X and Y' is more accurate and just as strong."* This is the load-bearing junior-tier register move. The flag names the choice ("the verb implies decision authority"), states the cost (interview probe will expose), and provides the rewrite (verb that's honest *and* still strong) — the candidate keeps the credit they earned without the credit they didn't.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Flags that read as accusation: "you're inflating scope," "you didn't actually lead this," "this is misleading." Even when the over-claim is real, the accusation register fails the no-roast brand promise. The candidate shuts down; the rewrite never gets adopted. Equally bad: the rubric soft-pedals and produces a flag that's so hedged ("you might want to consider whether 'led' is the right word here") that it doesn't surface the actual probe risk.

**Framework cite.** What/How/Why — the rewrite shape that lets the bullet keep its impact while honestly naming contribution.

**R-ID cites.** R10 (seniority mismatch), R12 (claiming team win solo).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md anti-roast 4-clause rule (§6) — names the choice not the person, states cost in concrete recruiter terms, provides rewrite, defaults to kindest plausible reading. This criterion is where the 4-clause rule gets tested hardest.

**Mid calibration: no delta.** The calibration-not-accusation register is identical at junior and mid — the 4-clause anti-roast rule (voice.md §6) applies the same way at both tiers, and the reframe move is the same shape. The only thing that shifts is the verb set the reframe targets (covered in §1, §2 deltas above). The reframe register itself does not differ across the junior/mid boundary; this criterion is the one place junior=mid in this rubric. Senior + staff register tightens further per `rubric-senior.md`.

## 4. Tool-name and method-name licensing at junior

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Naming tools (Figma, Maze, Mixpanel, Amplitude, dscout) and methods (semi-structured interviews, moderated usability testing, card sorts, affinity diagramming, journey mapping) is **appropriate at junior tier** — it signals craft fluency and seeds KSA matches honestly inside bullet context. Junior resumes earn credibility by demonstrating they know the tools and methods of the discipline; the bullet "Conducted 8 moderated usability tests in Maze on the v2 onboarding prototype" reads as junior-grade honest specificity.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** The license has limits. Method-listing without output is R11 (design process over-narration) — *"Conducted user interviews, ran usability testing, created journey maps, synthesized affinity diagrams, facilitated workshops, built personas, drafted wireframes…"* is method inventory, not accomplishment. The fix is to name the *single output* the methods produced. Equally: tools-in-skills-section noun salad is R05 — Figma + Sketch + Adobe XD + Miro + Notion + Jira + Linear + Slack + Trello + FigJam + Maze + UserTesting + Hotjar + Mixpanel + Amplitude + Looker reads as keyword-stuffing, not fluency.

**Framework cite.** XYZ — when an honest hard metric exists, the tool name lands inside the How clause: "Cut activation drop-off 38%→22% (X) by restructuring onboarding from 9 steps to 4, validated through 8 Maze tests with n=120 (Y)." CAR — when the tool/method is the load-bearing context.

**R-ID cites.** R05 (tool-list noun salad), R11 (design process over-narration), R01 (activity bullet — when method-listing replaces outcome).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md no-keyword-stuffing rule (§9.3 / §11.7) — keyword inclusion only via honest bullet rewrites that name actual tools used; never as an "add these words" list.

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, the tool-name license narrows. Naming Figma on every bullet on a mid resume reads as a downgrade signal — the mid reviewer is scanning for scope and outcome, and tool names that earned space at junior eat that real estate at mid. Method names compress further: instead of "conducted semi-structured interviews and synthesized affinity diagrams," mid bullets read "validated the [hypothesis] through 12 stakeholder interviews; the team killed the [feature] based on findings." The method becomes the How clause middle, not the headline. rubric-senior.md flags tool-name density as an over-narration tell at senior; rubric-junior.md keeps the license at junior, narrows it at mid.

## 5. Education-prominence rule (junior tier)

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** For 0–2 year resumes, recent education sits **above experience** when education is the most impressive signal on the page. STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md §D10 verbatim Nate (4tg1bPbOxus L1390): *"If you are a recent graduate, then oftentimes the most impressive thing you have on your resume is the education."* Bootcamp grads, HCI Masters, design-program BFAs with strong portfolio output: education-first is the right shape. The education block names degree, institution, graduation year, and one or two load-bearing details (capstone, GPA only if 3.7+, relevant coursework only if it maps to KSAs).

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Education-first **on a non-junior resume** is a tell — STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md §H4 / §H11 verbatim Nate (4tg1bPbOxus L1339-1340, L1398-1400): *"I typically want to see work experience before skills… My perception coming into this resume is that you've already been working on this for five years. And that's a more impressive stance than that of starting with, I just graduated six months ago."* Equally bad: education block padded with every coursework title, every certificate, every workshop — section bloat without signal.

**Framework cite.** Skim-test tiers — applied at the 0–3s tier (header read), the section-order check is "what's the strongest signal, and is it leading?"

**R-ID cites.** R09 (generic summary, wasted top-third — adjacent failure mode where junior wastes top-of-page real estate on filler).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md descending-importance discipline (Pair 3 — "anything in the middle of a paragraph basically gets lost forever — first and last is what people actually see").

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, education drops below experience — full stop. Three to five years of work experience makes the work the headline; the degree becomes a one-liner at the bottom. STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md §H11 verbatim Nate is the calibration anchor: experience-first reads as more impressive than recent-grad-first. The education block at mid compresses to degree + institution + year, no coursework, no GPA, no honors unless directly relevant. Section-order sins at mid: education above experience is a downgrade signal that positions the candidate at junior regardless of the years.

## 6. Projects-as-experience licensing (junior tier)

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Coursework, capstones, freelance, side projects, and unshipped work **roll into the experience section** with the same bullet format as employed roles. Three case-study-grade projects at 80% beat one polished employed bullet for first-job applications. The junior resume that separates "Projects" from "Experience" signals the candidate doesn't think projects count — and the reviewer reads the first signal: "she's discounting this." Roll them in; treat them as real work. Per `common-flags.md` R07-equivalent in portfolio-rx (concept ports here, anchored at junior tier): chronologically interleaved, same bullet shape, with project-context made clear ("Capstone — University of [X] HCI program; client: [nonprofit]; scope: 3-month sprint").

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Two failure shapes. (1) Junior resume with a polished employed-role bullet plus a fragmented "Projects" sidebar of half-described capstones — reads as the candidate hiding the work. Fix: roll into experience. (2) Junior resume that *over-claims* on a project — the freelance gig becomes "Owned the redesign for a 50-person company" when the actual scope was a logo refresh and a single landing page. Fix: scope precision. Both anti-patterns surface in the audit appendix; both port from common-flags.md R07 anchored at junior tier.

**Framework cite.** What/How/Why — bullet-shape consistency across employed and unemployed roles. STAR — useful as a thinking tool for recovering the full arc behind a coursework project before compressing to a resume bullet (rarely the right shape on the page itself).

**R-ID cites.** R01 (activity-only bullet — common failure when project bullets focus on coursework deliverables instead of outcomes), R12 (claiming team win solo — applies to group capstones).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md anti-roast §6 — when flagging a separated "Projects" section, frame as "this signals you don't think projects count," not "you're hiding work."

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, projects-as-experience licensing **narrows sharply**. A standalone "Projects" section on a mid resume signals coasting — the reviewer pattern-matches it as "this candidate didn't ship at the day job, so they're padding with side work." The mid resume's experience section should carry full weight on its own. Exceptions: a side project that became a real product (acquired, monetized, public adoption) earns its own bullet inside experience or in a one-line "selected work" footer. Otherwise, drop the projects section at mid. <!-- GAP: STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md does not have a verbatim Tim/Nate flag of mid-tier "Projects" section as a coasting tell — the corpus addresses this indirectly through R09-equivalent positioning critique. v1.1+ corpus mining should look for mid-track resume reviews that surface side-projects as a downgrade signal. -->

## 7. No-fabricated-metrics hard rule

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Junior bullets surface the absence of metrics as a **probe**, not as an invitation to invent. The honest options per voice.md §9.1 escalation chain: (1) hard metric with sourcing — "38% → 22% drop-off, Mixpanel Q3 2024, n=14k"; (2) soft metric / behavior change — "support tickets on the affected flow dropped from ~30/wk to ~5/wk"; (3) prototyped with numbers — "comparable redesigns ship 15-25% engagement lift; ours likely sits in that band"; (4) honest scope — "led seven plus projects." Maturity beats round percentages every time. SPEC D-16-RR is hard rule: never invent a metric the user did not provide.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** Two anti-patterns. (1) Suspiciously round percentages on un-instrumented work — "Increased engagement by 45%" on a student project with no real users. Hiring managers identify fabricated metrics on sight; the candidate looks like they don't know the difference between real measurement and made-up numbers. (2) Vague platitudes — "improved the experience," "delighted users," "drove engagement," "elevated the product," "delivered impact," "moved the needle," "transformed the workflow." These commit to nothing. They're filler. Junior resumes are the highest-fab-risk surface — the rubric's job is to surface absence as a probe, never to invite invention.

**Framework cite.** XYZ — when an honest hard metric exists. CAR or Scope-Impact-Metric — when no honest metric exists, downgrade to a scope-led shape rather than fabricating into XYZ's metric slot.

**R-ID cites.** R02 (metric fabrication risk, no honest signal) — load-bearing for this criterion. R01 (activity bullet, no outcome) — adjacent failure mode where the bullet skips the outcome entirely rather than fabricating one.

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md no-fabricated-metrics rule (§9.1 / §11.2) — verbatim hard rule, source D-16-RR. Abstraction-regression guard (§4) — rewrites cannot introduce a fake number that wasn't in the original.

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, the metric expectation **lifts**. Junior tier accepts "verb + result + any measurable signal." Mid tier closes the loophole: every bullet has a metric or a defensible scope signal. Soft metric license narrows — at mid, behavior-change / shipped-artifact outcomes are still valid, but at least 50% of bullets need hard metrics (not a strict count, a directional rule). The no-fab rule is identical at both tiers — never invent a number — but the mid bar pushes harder on probing the user for what *could* have been measured before falling back to scope-only language.

## 8. Honest non-outcome calibration

**What good looks like (junior baseline).** Junior tier carries an explicit license to **lead with honest non-outcome** when projects didn't ship, didn't get measured, or are too early. The shape: "Killed by leadership pivot — what I learned: should have stress-tested executive alignment before sketching." Or: "Internal tool, no instrumentation; planned to track adoption and time-to-first-action next." Or: "Shipped to internal users only, no external metrics; team adopted the empty-state pattern across two adjacent surfaces." Honest non-outcome reads as more mature than fabricated metrics — it signals the candidate understands what measurement actually means.

**What doesn't (junior failure mode).** The non-outcome license is **not** an excuse for omission. A bullet that just says "Designed the feature" without naming why it didn't ship, what was learned, or what observable behavior change occurred is back to R01 territory (activity, no outcome). The non-outcome bullet has to do real work — name the killed-by reason, the learned-from beat, or the soft signal — otherwise it's just an activity bullet hiding behind humility. Equally bad: leading every bullet with non-outcome when honest measurable signals *do* exist for some of the work — under-claiming on the bullets that have real metrics in service of consistency.

**Framework cite.** What/How/Why — the non-outcome bullet still flips: lead with the *what changed* (even if it's a learning, a process change, or a behavior shift), then how, then what. R-A-S can also frame an honest non-outcome where the Result clause becomes the learning or the post-killed reflection.

**R-ID cites.** R01 (activity bullet, no outcome — what the non-outcome license tries to prevent), R02 (metric fabrication risk — what the non-outcome license replaces with honesty).

**Voice-rule cites.** voice.md no-fabricated-metrics rule (§9.1) — the honest-non-outcome shape is the explicit fallback when even prototyped numbers aren't available. Anti-roast §6 — naming a project as killed without naming the team or company as the cause (kindest plausible reading).

**Mid calibration note (folded inline).** At mid, honest non-outcome bullets are **still licensed** but used more sparingly. The mid resume's experience section should carry mostly metric-bearing or scope-bearing bullets, with non-outcome reserved for genuinely unshipped or NDA-blocked work. The shape shifts slightly: at mid, the honest-non-outcome bullet adds a *what I would do differently* beat that demonstrates senior-grade self-awareness. Per STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md P-RES-06 (trade-off probe at the resume scope): "On this bullet, what did you give up to get the result you're claiming? What didn't ship that you wanted to? Would you make the same trade again?" Mid honest-non-outcome answers that probe directly inside the bullet.

## Calibration note discipline (per LD-P2-06)

The seven mid calibration notes above are folded **inline at each criterion**, not collected at the file end. This is structural per D-18 and LD-P2-06: a workflow consuming this file walks the criteria sequentially and reads junior-then-mid for each, never "all junior then all mid." If a criterion's mid behavior is identical to junior (no delta), the calibration note explicitly says so — silence is not the rule. If a future delta surfaces, it goes inline at the relevant criterion, not in a new section.

The seven mid calibration deltas distributed above (criterion §3 is the explicit no-delta — calibration register is identical at junior/mid):

1. **§1 verb shift** — junior (*Designed, Built, Conducted*) → mid (*Led, Owned, Drove, Shipped with metric*); solo contribution explicit; junior set is downgrade signal at mid.
2. **§2 over-claiming sharpens at mid** — failure mode shifts from junior over-claim ("pretending to be senior") to mid over-claim ("vague leadership verbs without scope evidence"); probe set verbatim per PITFALLS §1.5.
3. **§3 no delta** — calibration-not-accusation register is identical at junior and mid.
4. **§4 tool/method license narrows at mid** — junior license to name tools is a downgrade signal at mid; method names compress to How-clause middle, not headline.
5. **§5 education drops below experience at mid** — section-order flip is the delta; mid-tier education compresses to one-line.
6. **§6 projects-as-experience license narrows sharply at mid** — standalone Projects section signals coasting at mid; experience section carries full weight.
7. **§7 metric expectation lifts at mid** — junior accepts soft signals; mid closes the loophole; ≥50% bullets need hard metrics directionally.
8. **§8 honest non-outcome stays licensed but compresses at mid** — adds what-I'd-do-differently beat per P-RES-06 trade-off probe.

Top-3 area-of-work labels also shift across the junior→mid boundary (per FEATURES §1.7, voice.md §11.8): at junior, *positioning* and *summary line* dominate; at mid, *bullet impact*, *scope claims*, and *seniority calibration* dominate. Same area-of-work template (per voice.md §3), different label weighting. The routing exit at mid is target-role-based: if the user is mid track but applying to senior-titled or promotion-track-staff roles, route to `references/rubric-senior.md` — the boundary is target role, not years-of-experience.
