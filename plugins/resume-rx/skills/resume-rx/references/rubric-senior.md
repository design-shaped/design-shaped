# Rubric — Senior + Staff resumes

> Diagnostic. Read the senior resume for target-role string, role-family alignment, and seniority calibration. Don't prescribe.

## Scope

This file covers **senior (TGT-03)** as the baseline tier and folds **staff (TGT-04)** as inline calibration notes per D-18 / LD-P2-06 — staff is not a separate section. Where the bar shifts at staff, a "Calibration at staff" note sits inline next to the parallel senior content. Junior and mid tiers live in `rubric-junior.md`; the two files never load together — `SKILL.md` routes by career stage, and the workflow loads exactly one rubric per review.

The senior failure mode resume-rx leads with is **under-claiming** (FEATURES §1.4 R10, FEATURES §1.7 senior gradient row, PITFALLS §1.6) — IC-shaped bullets that hide leadership scope, mentorship, hiring-loop work, and cross-org influence the candidate did but didn't write down. Junior resumes fail in repeating, prescriptive ways; senior resumes fail in individual ways. The diagnostic register is calibration, not accusation — voice.md Pair 7 frames the move.

The senior tell sits in the seams: scope language in role-line housekeeping, bullet-level second-order effects, and the through-line across roles. The work itself is usually competent. The diagnostic asks whether the seams add up to a coherent senior thesis or generalist drift.

## Criterion 1 — What-changes-because-of-you diagnostic

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** Every senior bullet answers the verbatim probe from voice.md §11.3 / §5: *"what changed because of you?"* — what was different about the product, team, or org after this work that wouldn't have been different if someone else had been in the seat. The bullet names the change, not just the activity. Per FEATURES §2.6, this is the diagnostic that surfaces senior-grade bullets from mid-grade bullets carrying senior titles.

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** Bullets read as activity descriptions — "led the design system rollout across three product teams" — and stop at scope without naming the change the work produced. The recruiter scans the bullet and learns what the candidate *did*, not what was *different afterward*. This is the under-claiming pattern (FEATURES §1.4 R10, common-flags.md R10) compounded with R01 activity-bullet failure: at senior level, the second-order effect is the bullet's headline signal.

**Frameworks.** Apply **What/How/Why** with the senior optimization — lead with the Why (what changed), then the How (the leadership move), then the What (the activity). For bullets where the change is itself a scope claim, **Scope-Impact-Metric** carries the load: scope context at senior is multi-team, multi-quarter, or org-capability-level.

**R-IDs.** R02 (no-fab metrics — never invent the change to fill the slot), R10 (seniority mismatch — under-claiming variant), R11 (process over-narration — when the change is buried under method roster).

**Voice rules.** Senior-bullet diagnostic (voice.md §11.3, §5 *what-changes-because-of-you* idiom). No-fab metrics (voice.md §11.2 / §9.1 / D-16-RR) — surface absence as a probe, never invent a number. Abstraction-regression guard (voice.md §4) — rewrites at least as concrete as the original.

**Calibration at staff.** The change must reach the **org level**, not the team level. Staff bullets answer: what was different about the *organization's capability* after this work? Per FEATURES §1.7 staff row: *"Verb + org-level scope + strategic narrative. Metrics tied to business outcomes, not feature outcomes."* A staff bullet whose second-order effect is "team adopted the pattern" is reading senior; the staff bar is "three teams adopted the pattern, the design org standardized on it, and the next quarter's roadmap planning surfaced the dependency for the first time."

## Criterion 2 — Senior gradient row (verb + scope noun + metric + second-order effect)

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** Per FEATURES §1.7 senior row verbatim: *"Verb + scope noun (team size / eng ratio / customer base) + metric + second-order effect."* The bullet shape carries four beats — a scope verb (led, owned, shipped, scaled, drove, partnered, established, restructured, defined), a scope noun with specific numbers (12-engineer pod, 4M MAU, 3 product surfaces), a metric (drop-off delta, adoption count, time-to-ship), and a second-order effect (the library that outlived the project, the standard adopted across teams, the partnership that survived the org change).

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** Bullets stop at three beats — verb + scope + metric — without the second-order effect. Or worse, bullets stop at two beats — verb + scope, no metric, no after-effect — and the recruiter reads "led design for a 12-engineer team" without ever finding out what the leadership produced. Generic scope words ("high-impact," "transformative," "cross-functional") substituted for specific numbers (cf. R09 generic-summary failure mode applied at bullet scope).

**Frameworks.** **Scope-Impact-Metric** is the explicit senior shape — scope context leads, impact follows, metric anchors. Pair with **What/How/Why** when the second-order effect is the load-bearing Why. **R-A-S** when the result earns the headline slot and the action+skill clauses can compress.

**R-IDs.** R02 (suspicious round percentages on multi-team scope claims), R08 ("we" overuse — the metric came from the team but the candidate framed it as personal), R12 (claiming team win solo — the scope-noun inflation variant).

**Voice rules.** Banned AI-default verbs (voice.md §11.1) — *spearheaded, leveraged, synergized, catalyzed* are the giveaway when a senior tries to compensate for thin scope language by reaching for inflation verbs. Scope-evidence-without-bragging (voice.md §5 keep-list) — specific numbers don't read as bragging; vague superlatives read as filler.

**Calibration at staff.** The scope noun shifts from team-level to **org-level**. Staff bullets reference org capability ("design org of 24"), multi-team headcount ("partnered across 6 product squads"), OKRs influenced ("shaped 3 quarterly OKRs"), or business-outcome metrics ($-denominated revenue lift, retention delta, sales-cycle compression) — not single-team metrics. Per FEATURES §1.7 staff row direct quote: *"Metrics tied to business outcomes, not feature outcomes."* Multi-team / cross-org influence becomes the dominant scope vocabulary at staff (PITFALLS §1.6 — staff under-claiming = senior under-claiming amplified, and the scope-noun is where the amplification lands).

## Criterion 3 — Under-claiming probe set

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** Senior resumes surface leadership-scope evidence even when it lives outside core IC work. Specifically, the resume names — somewhere — at least three of the four under-claiming probes: **mentorship** (designers mentored, juniors leveled, hiring panels participated in), **cross-org influence** (RFCs authored, standards adopted by other teams, design-org systems built), **process standards-setting** (review processes owned, design crit cadence built, design-eng partnership established), and **hiring / interview-loop work** (panels run, rubrics authored, designers hired). Per PITFALLS §1.6, these are the four areas senior candidates reliably did the work in but reliably failed to write down.

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** The candidate's resume reads as IC-only — every bullet is a craft verb, every role is a project list, and the leadership-scope evidence the candidate genuinely produced is invisible. The recruiter scans the resume and sees a strong mid-level designer. This is the highest-leverage senior failure mode resume-rx surfaces (PITFALLS §1.6 prevention strategy: *"Rubric-senior.md defines what scope signals belong at senior and staff levels"*).

**Frameworks.** **Skim-test tiers** runs first to surface where the under-claiming dies on the page — typically the 3–10s tier (recent role, top bullet) is where the leadership-scope evidence should be loudest, and isn't. **Scope-Impact-Metric** carries the rewrite: surface the under-claimed evidence at proper scope.

**R-IDs.** R10 (seniority mismatch — under-claiming variant) is the exact pattern this criterion targets. R11 (process over-narration) often masks under-claiming — designers fill bullets with method inventories instead of leadership-scope evidence.

**Voice rules.** Senior-bullet diagnostic (voice.md §11.3, Pair 7) — frame the surfacing as positioning miss, not maturity miss. Voice.md §6.1 cost-framing applies — the cost is "recruiter scans senior resume, reads IC verbs, discards from senior pile."

**Calibration at staff.** Same four probes, amplified. **Mentorship** at staff means staff-level mentorship — leveling other senior designers up to staff, not leveling juniors up to mid. **Cross-org influence** at staff means RFCs adopted across the org or industry, not standards adopted by neighboring teams. **Process standards-setting** at staff means org-wide design-engineering partnership models, design-system governance, hiring-rubric ownership across the design org. **Hiring / interview-loop work** at staff means owning the design hiring loop, calibrating leveling rubrics, contributing to compensation bands. The pattern is the same; the scope is org-level.

## Criterion 4 — Craft-verb-dominant flag

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** Scope verbs (Led, owned, shipped, scaled, drove, partnered, established, restructured, defined) appear at least as often as craft verbs (designed, prototyped, researched, wireframed, illustrated). The bullets across the most-recent role read as a leadership story with craft work attached, not a craft portfolio with leadership claims attached. Per the audit appendix Step 3 carry: scope verbs land first, craft verbs land in the How clause.

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** Every bullet across the most-recent role opens with *Designed / Prototyped / Wireframed / Researched / Created / Crafted*. Read together, the bullets argue for a strong IC craft contributor — which positions the candidate at mid regardless of years (FEATURES §1.7 senior row). This is the warning sign PITFALLS §1.6 names: *"craft-dominant bullets where every verb is designed / prototyped / researched without leadership-scope language."* The flag fires when 4 of 5 bullets in the most-recent role lead with a craft verb.

**Frameworks.** **What/How/Why** flips: the craft verb belongs in the How clause, not the headline slot. **R-A-S** at senior tier when the result is leadable — the result-led shape forces a non-craft-verb opening.

**R-IDs.** R10 (seniority mismatch — bullet voice wrong for level — the craft-dominant variant). R04 (passive / weak verb) when the craft verb itself is the weakest part — *"Designed wireframes for the checkout flow"* combines R10 and R04.

**Voice rules.** Banned AI-default verbs (voice.md §11.1) — when a candidate notices the craft-verb-dominance and tries to fix it by reaching for *spearheaded / leveraged / orchestrated*, the rewrite lost. Honest scope verbs (voice.md §5, frameworks.md Scope-Impact-Metric definition): *led, owned, shipped, scaled, drove, partnered, established, restructured, defined*.

**Calibration at staff.** At staff, craft-verb dominance is a louder failure — staff bullets that lead with craft verbs read as "former senior designer who got promoted on title alone." The staff bar requires scope verbs paired with org-level scope nouns (Criterion 2 staff calibration). Where a staff candidate's most-recent role is genuinely IC-craft-heavy (an embedded staff designer at a small org, a returning-from-management staff designer in their first IC quarter), the framing is explicit: *"Returning to IC after 18 months managing — first senior IC role at [company], craft contributions during ramp"* — surface the manager-pivot framing rather than letting the craft verbs accumulate without explanation.

## Criterion 5 — Second-order effect probe

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** At least one bullet per role surfaces a **second-order effect** — what outlived the project. Per FEATURES §2.6 worked example: *"pattern adopted across 3 product surfaces, produced a reusable Auth flow library."* The second-order effect can be a reusable artifact (library, component, pattern), an organizational artifact (process, standard, hiring rubric), a behavior change (team adopted, org standardized), or a downstream outcome (the next quarter's roadmap moved because of this work). Per the audit appendix Step 4 fold: senior output gets smaller, the decision trail and the after-effect get bigger.

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** Bullets terminate at the metric — *"Reduced drop-off from 38% to 22%"* — and never name what outlived the metric. The recruiter reads the bullet and learns the project worked; they don't learn what was different about the org afterward. At senior, the metric is the floor; the second-order effect is the ceiling.

**Frameworks.** **Scope-Impact-Metric** with the third beat doing the second-order work — the M slot in S-I-M is often a behavior-change or adoption-count metric, not the primary outcome metric. **What/How/Why** with the Why expanded to cover the after-effect (*"...and the pattern outlived the project as a reusable Auth library"* sits inside the Why beat).

**R-IDs.** R02 (no-fab metrics — when no real second-order effect exists, surface absence as a probe; never invent the library that wasn't built). R10 (seniority mismatch — the variant where the candidate has the second-order effect but didn't write it down).

**Voice rules.** No-fab metrics (voice.md §11.2 / §9.1) extends to the second-order effect — never fabricate the adoption-count, the surface-spread, or the survival of the artifact. Soft-metric / behavior-change framing (voice.md §9.1 escalation chain) carries when the after-effect is qualitative — *"the pattern became the team's default for new flows"* is honest scope without numbers.

**Calibration at staff.** The second-order effect must reach **multi-team or org level**. Staff after-effects are: process changes adopted by the design org, hiring-rubric standards picked up across leveling bands, design-eng partnership models replicated, business-strategy shifts the work surfaced. A staff bullet whose after-effect is "two designers on my team adopted the pattern" is reading senior. The staff bar is "the design org standardized on this pattern, and three subsequent product launches inherited the partnership model."

## Criterion 6 — Bullet-level title-portability check (P-RES-07)

**What good looks like (senior baseline).** When the candidate's previous title is environment-dependent (Design Lead, Senior Designer, Principal Designer at orgs with non-standard leveling), the resume bullets carry the scope evidence to translate the title across environments. Per STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md P-RES-07 verbatim probe: *"Your previous title was [X]. Does that title mean the same thing in a centralized environment vs decentralized? At the company you're targeting, will their recruiter read this title the way your previous company meant it?"* The bullets do the translation work the title can't.

**What doesn't (senior failure mode).** The candidate's title was Design Lead at a centralized environment (managerial, design-organization-shaped) and they're applying to a decentralized environment (embedded on product teams, PM-partnered, sprint-shaped). The bullets read as their old environment, not their new target — and the title-portability gap reads as positioning miss to recruiters who run differently. STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md §E5 (Nate verbatim, 11zGHBSfaXc L660–670): *"Design lead means something different depending on the environment. There are design leads that are managerial in nature… there are design leads that are dedicated toward individual product teams."* The bullets need to disambiguate.

**Frameworks.** **Skim-test tiers** runs first — the 0–3s test surfaces when the title creates a positioning mismatch the bullets need to compensate for. **CAR** (frameworks.md) carries when one-clause Context disambiguates the environment shape ("On an embedded product squad with PM + 4 engineers...").

**R-IDs.** R10 (seniority mismatch — the title-portability variant, where the scope-words in the bullets don't match the title's meaning at the target environment). R09 (generic summary) often compounds the failure when a generic summary doesn't position the title's meaning.

**Voice rules.** Voice.md §6.1 *naming the limit of expertise* — for non-US ATS landscapes, title conventions vary; flag when the rubric's environment assumptions don't transfer. Voice.md Pair 4 (coherence across the resume) — when the title and the bullets argue for different environments, name the positioning miss directly.

**Calibration at staff.** Title inflation/deflation arc check is load-bearing at staff. A candidate whose titles read non-monotonic across roles (Senior at Series-A → Staff at Series-D → Senior at FAANG) needs framing — the staff title at a Series-D may carry weight that the FAANG Senior doesn't, and recruiters who don't know the org context misread the arc as a downgrade. The framing is explicit at the role line — *"Returning to the FAANG senior IC track after a Series-D staff stint; total IC scope across the two roles equivalent to FAANG L6"* — rather than letting the title arc speak for itself. Manager-pivot framing extends the same logic — staff candidates returning to IC from a brief management stint surface the move at the role line, not in the bullets. STREAM_MINING_RESUME.md §D11 (verbatim Nate, ox8cqMpZbEg L331–332) anchors this discipline: *"It's pretty smart to put your senior product designer here at the top rather than sabbatical, even though the years don't line up."*

## Calibration-note placement protocol

Per D-18 / LD-P2-06: staff calibration notes appear **inline** next to the parallel senior content, never in a separate "Staff" section. Reviewers consuming this rubric apply the senior baseline first, then read the inline calibration note for the same criterion if the candidate is targeting staff. The seven staff calibration notes distributed across Criteria 1–6 are: (1) org-level change, not team-level, in Criterion 1; (2) org-level scope vocabulary in Criterion 2; (3) staff-level mentorship + cross-org RFCs + org-wide standards-setting + design-org hiring-loop work in Criterion 3; (4) craft-verb dominance is a louder failure at staff + manager-pivot framing in Criterion 4; (5) multi-team / org-level after-effects in Criterion 5; (6) title inflation/deflation arc + manager-pivot framing in Criterion 6; (7) calibration-note placement protocol itself, documented here.

The sensitivity callout: when the rubric's senior-vs-staff calibration would land as accusation rather than calibration ("you're claiming staff but the bullets read senior"), apply voice.md Pair 7 and §6.1 — frame as positioning miss, not maturity miss; surface the gap as an opportunity to clarify target, not as a verdict on the candidate's level.

## Top-3 wrap area-of-work labels

Per voice.md §3 / §11.8 the senior+staff Top-3 wrap uses the resume-domain area-of-work template with the FEATURES area-of-work labels: **bullet impact, scope claims, role-family alignment, seniority calibration, summary line**. (Plus *ATS-readability, positioning, housekeeping* per voice.md §3.) Drop *layout* and *visual craft* — resume-rx is content-only per D-15-RR. Variable count 1–3 per voice.md §3 / Pattern C — most senior+staff Top-3s resolve to 2 priorities, sometimes 1; never manufacture a third.

Senior + staff priority order, when applicable:

1. **Seniority calibration** (if the candidate's most-recent role bullets read mid or junior despite the title) — always position #1; without it, the rest of the review is sand. This is where the under-claiming probe set (Criterion 3) and the craft-verb-dominant flag (Criterion 4) most often surface as Top-3 priority #1.
2. **Bullet impact** (the single highest-leverage bullet rewrite) — specific bullet quoted verbatim, the rewrite handed back via What/How/Why or Scope-Impact-Metric.
3. **Scope claims** or **summary line** (whichever is the dominant secondary failure mode for this candidate) — specific, named.

Resume reviews can be MORE direct in the Top-3 at senior+staff — the audience has earned bluntness. But "no roasting" still holds (voice.md §6 anti-roast hard rule).

## Calibration-note discipline reminder

Per LD-P2-06 / D-18: every staff calibration note in this file is **folded inline** next to the parallel senior content. Reviewers must not consolidate the staff notes into a separate "Staff section" — the calibration-by-criterion structure is what makes the rubric usable in a single read for senior reviewers and a senior+calibration read for staff reviewers. If a future edit feels the urge to extract the staff notes into a separate section, that's the signal that the senior baseline content needs strengthening at that criterion, not that the staff notes need separating.

Senior resumes fail in individual ways. Apply this rubric as a diagnostic, not a checklist. If a review is producing senior-feedback that would apply to any senior, the workflow is doing junior-shaped feedback in senior clothing — back up and re-extract the target-role string and role-family signal before continuing.
