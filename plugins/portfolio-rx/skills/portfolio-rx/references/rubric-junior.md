# Rubric — Junior portfolio + resume review

> Prescriptive. Tell juniors what to fix in what order.
> No roasting: just collaboration and constructive feedback.
> Source: 23 @designshaped Friday-stream transcripts. Joint Tim+Nate voice.
> Voice register: `references/voice.md`. Every flag in this file complies with `voice.md` §3 (pattern templates), §4 (drop list), §6 (anti-roast hard rule).

---

## Who this rubric is for

Junior, early-career, not-yet-on-promotion-track designers: bootcamp grads, career switchers, 0–2 years of design experience, self-taught designers, HCI Masters grads, students. Job-search contexts: building a first portfolio, actively applying, post-interview-flop, re-entering after a gap.

This rubric is **prescriptive** (per SPEC D-14). Junior portfolios fail in repeating ways. We push hard on the common failures here. We tell users what to fix and in what order.

For mid → senior promotion-track designers, route to `references/rubric-senior.md`. That rubric is diagnostic — it extracts the user's thesis and grades against it. Don't apply both. Pick one based on the career-stage routing in SKILL.md.

The big picture, before any section runs: junior portfolios almost always need positioning before they need polish. A junior with three half-finished case studies and a sharp value prop beats a junior with one polished case study and a generic hero every time. Walk the sections in order.

## Section 1 — Positioning (the load-bearing junior diagnostic)

The first question every junior portfolio has to answer, in five seconds, on the homepage: **what kind of designer are you?**

Most junior portfolios fail here, which means everything downstream is moot. Don't move past this section until positioning lands.

### What to look for

- A hero sentence that names: who you are (specialty + level), who it's for (target audience), and the benefit you deliver. Apply the **copy-paste test** — if the line could be lifted onto another designer's portfolio and still read as true, it's positioning anyone, which means it's positioning no one.
- A posture across the body of work. Three or more competing titles or industries with equal weight = no posture.
- A "why design" lens that's specific. "I love creativity" is anyone. "I make B2B feel less hostile" is someone.

### Apply the framework: Three Target Audiences

Walk the portfolio through the three readers (cite by name — see `frameworks-stream.md#three-target-audiences`):

- **Recruiter** — skims the homepage value prop and the case-study housekeeping for KSA keyword match. Doesn't read narrative.
- **Hiring manager** — reads case studies for problem framing, decisions, process.
- **Future peers** — reads the about page to decide whether they'd want to work with you.

Most junior portfolios optimize for the hiring manager and skip the recruiter and future-peer audiences. The recruiter is the gate. The future peer is the referral channel. Both have to be addressed before the hiring manager pass even matters.

### Flags

- `Element: the hero. Cost: "Hi, I'm [name], a passionate designer creating user-centered digital experiences" passes the copy-paste test for any other portfolio — it's positioning anyone, which positions no one. Recruiter bounces in five seconds. Fix: write a value-prop sentence with all four parts — who you are, what you do, who it's for, the benefit. "Junior product designer. I ship onboarding flows for B2B SaaS. Targeting first product-design roles at Series A–B startups." See: frameworks-stream.md#three-target-audiences.`
- `Element: the body of work. Cost: SaaS dashboard + e-comm checkout + fitness app + B2B onboarding reads as "I'll take any role." Recruiter can't pattern-match you to a posting. Fix: pick a posture and put it in the hero. "I make complex B2B feel calm" or "I ship small consumer tools fast" — any specific posture beats the unstated everything-shape. The cut categories don't disappear; they get demoted to a "selected work" footnote. See: frameworks-nate.md#brand-drivers.`
- `Element: the about page. Cost: stock-photo headshot + one paragraph that could be on any designer's site. The future-peer audience can't tell what kind of teammate you'd be — and referrals are the strongest hiring channel. Fix: write the about page as a dating piece in a professional register. What you read, what you're learning, what kind of teams you do well in. Mash professional history into it (referrals look at both at once). See: frameworks-stream.md#three-target-audiences.`

## Section 2 — Housekeeping at the top of every case study

A factual block at the top of every case study, separate from the narrative. It's what the recruiter reads first; it's what tells the hiring manager whether to keep scrolling.

This is a senior-tell at junior level. Most junior portfolios skip housekeeping entirely. Add it and the credibility floor moves up immediately.

### What to look for

Every case study opens with a housekeeping block that names:

- **Role** on the project (sole designer / lead designer / one of N designers)
- **Timeline** (Q3 2024, 4 months)
- **Industry** (B2B SaaS / healthcare / fintech / etc.)
- **Team composition** (1 PM, 3 engineers, 1 researcher)
- **Tools used** (Figma, Maze, Mixpanel)
- **Link** out to the live work, where applicable

Apply the framework: **Housekeeping** (cite by name — see `frameworks-stream.md#housekeeping`). Pairs with **KSAs** — housekeeping is where you seed the keywords the recruiter's ATS is scanning for.

### Flags

- `Element: the [project name] case study. Cost: opens directly on the problem statement. Recruiter has no factual anchor — can't tell what role you held, what tools, what scale. Fix: add a 5-line housekeeping block at the top. Role / timeline / industry / team / tools. Reads in five seconds; lets the recruiter triage. See: frameworks-stream.md#housekeeping.`
- `Element: housekeeping language across case studies. Cost: every case study lists the same three tools and "Designer." Reads as boilerplate. Fix: differentiate by what was actually true on each project. If you owned interaction design on one and visual on another, name that. The housekeeping is part of the case study's argument, not a template field.`

## Section 3 — The Read-the-Titles-Only Test

The diagnostic for whether a case study earns its scroll. Most case studies fail this test silently — the section titles read as checkbox labels ("Overview," "Research," "Process," "Final Design") instead of carrying the story.

### How to run the test

Strip out all body content from the case study. Read **only the section titles**, top to bottom. If the titles tell the story by themselves, the case study passes. If the titles are format names that don't carry narrative, the case study fails.

Apply the framework: **Read-the-Titles-Only Test** (cite by name — see `frameworks-stream.md#titles-only-test`). The companion is **Children's Picture Book Rhetoric** — the case study should read closer to a picture book than an essay. Long content is fine; long *paragraphs* are not.

### What to look for

- Section titles that summarize the section's argument, not the format.
- "Overview" → "Why we picked opinionated primitives over a federated kit."
- "Research" → "Eight drivers told us the same thing about handoffs."
- "Solution" → "What we shipped that finally moved the number."

### Flags

- `Element: the section titles in the [project name] case study. Cost: titles read "Overview / Research / Process / Wireframes / Final Design / Impact." Hiring managers skim — they read titles, not paragraphs. Fix: rewrite each title as a story-bearing sentence. "Intro" → "Who is [project user]?", "Solution" → "What we shipped that finally moved the number." Apply the titles-only test: read the titles top to bottom; if they tell the story, you're done. See: frameworks-stream.md#titles-only-test.`
- `Element: the [project name] case study form factor. Cost: case study is mostly long paragraphs with few visuals — text leaks past 1000+ pixels wide and the reader bounces before the work registers. Fix: convert paragraphs into annotated images, captioned diagrams, before/after screenshots. Constrain body copy to ~600–800px wide. The image carries 80% of the load; the text supports. See: frameworks-stream.md#picture-book.`

## Section 4 — Why-How-What bullet rewriting

Every resume bullet, every case-study TLDR sentence, every hero line — apply this scaffold. It's the single most-cited framework across the stream corpus and the universal scaffold for impact-first writing.

### How to apply

Bullets default to **What → How → Why** order: "Designed wireframes for the checkout flow using Figma to improve conversion." Activity first, impact last.

Flip to **Why → How → What**: "Cut checkout drop-off from 38% to 22% by redesigning the address-confirmation step in Figma." Impact first, method middle, artifact last.

Apply the framework: **What/How/Why ↔ Why/How/What** (cite by name — see `frameworks-stream.md#what-how-why`). Headline rule, recurring across streams: *"if you follow a what-how-why approach, really hard to write a bad bullet."*

### What to look for

- Every bullet leads with a result (number, behavior change, shipped artifact, scope), not an activity.
- Where hard metrics don't exist, the bullet leads with a **soft metric** (behavior change, adoption, scope) — see Section 5.
- The hero sentence and the case-study TLDR both lead with the same impact-first ordering.

### Flags

- `Bullet says: "Designed wireframes for the checkout flow." Diagnostic: what was the result? Rewrite per Why/How/What: "Cut checkout drop-off 38% → 22% by redesigning the address-confirmation step (Mixpanel, Q3 2024, n=12K)." Why first. See: frameworks-stream.md#what-how-why.`
- `Bullet says: "Led end-to-end design for AI questionnaire workflows." Diagnostic: led to what? Rewrite per Why/How/What: "Improved efficiency 7x by leading end-to-end design for AI questionnaire workflows (internal tool, ~50 users)." Most impressive thing first. See: frameworks-stream.md#what-how-why.`
- `Element: the [project name] case-study TLDR. Cost: opens with "This project explored how to redesign the onboarding flow." Activity-first; reader has no reason to keep going. Fix per Why/How/What: lead with the outcome. "Cut activation time from 12 minutes to 4 minutes by collapsing onboarding from 9 steps to 4." Why first, then expand into how and what. See: frameworks-stream.md#what-how-why.`

## Section 5 — Outcome before metric

The outcome paragraph leads with the customer-facing change, then the metric — not the other way around. And honest non-outcomes beat fabricated metrics every time.

### What to look for

Every case study lands one of three outcome shapes:

1. **Hard metric** — a real number with a real source. "Cut checkout drop-off 38% → 22%, Mixpanel Q3 2024, n=12K sessions."
2. **Soft metric / behavior change** — a shipped artifact, a user signal, a real consequence. "Shipped Q3 2024 to ~50K MAU; support tickets on the affected flow dropped from ~30/wk to ~5/wk." Or: "The team adopted this as the default pattern across three surfaces."
3. **Honest non-outcome** — for projects that didn't ship, didn't get measured, or are too early. "Killed by leadership pivot — what I learned: should have stress-tested executive alignment before sketching." Or: "Internal tool, no instrumentation; planned to track adoption and time-to-first-action next."

Apply the framework: **Hard Metrics vs Soft Metrics (Behavior Change)** (cite by name — see `frameworks-stream.md#hard-soft-metrics`). When neither exists and the project is conceptual or NDA-blocked, escalate to **Prototyping with Numbers** (see `frameworks-stream.md#prototyping-with-numbers`).

### The drop list (banned outcome verbs, per voice.md §4)

- "improved the experience"
- "delighted users"
- "drove engagement"
- "elevated the product"
- "delivered impact"
- "moved the needle"
- "transformed the workflow"

These commit to nothing. They're filler.

### Flags

- `Element: the outcome paragraph in the [project name] case study. Cost: outcome lives in paragraph 6. Hiring manager skims, sees process, leaves before the result. Fix: tease the outcome at the top of the case study, then expand it at the bottom. The customer-facing change leads ("checkout completion went from frustrating to one-tap"); the metric supports ("38% → 22% drop-off"). See: frameworks-stream.md#hook-proof-story.`
- `Element: the [project name] case study outcome. Cost: claims "45% engagement lift" on a student project with no real users — round-number metrics on un-instrumented work get clocked instantly. Fix: replace with what you'd have measured ("primary signal: task completion rate; secondary: time-on-task; n target: 30 over 2 weeks") + one honest sentence on why this didn't generate real data. Honesty signals more maturity than fake numbers. See: frameworks-stream.md#hard-soft-metrics.`
- `Element: the [project name] case study. Cost: shipped to internal users, no instrumentation cited, outcome paragraph reads "users responded positively." Fix: name the soft metric concretely. "Shipped Q3 2024; the support team flagged a 60% drop in tickets on this flow that quarter; team adopted the empty-state pattern across two adjacent surfaces." Behavior change is a valid outcome shape. See: frameworks-stream.md#hard-soft-metrics.`

## Section 6 — Three projects > one polished

The junior portfolio-count heuristic. If you have one project, that's a problem — but the fix isn't to ship a brand-new fourth project from scratch. The fix is to write up coursework, freelance, or unshipped work you already did.

### Why this matters

For first-job applications, three case studies at 80% beat one at 99%. The cost of the missing 20% on each is small. The cost of three missing case studies is large. (See `frameworks-nate.md#the-80-percent-principle` for the underlying heuristic.)

For pivot-track and bootcamp portfolios, the failure mode is usually one polished case study (the bootcamp capstone) plus three fragments. Push the three fragments to 80%; ship all four. Don't keep polishing the polished one.

### What to look for

- 3–5 case studies, not 1, not 10+.
- Each case study has its own argument — different problem shape, different decisions, different posture-evidence.
- Coursework, freelance, side projects, and unshipped work all rolled into the same case-study format as employed work. Treat them as real work; the maturity in the framing matters more than the contract status.

### Flags

- `Element: the case-study count. Cost: one polished case study, no others. Reads as "I'm not ready yet." Fix: write up coursework, freelance, or unshipped work as additional case studies. Three case studies at 80% beats one at 99% for first-job applications. The fastest fix isn't another portfolio piece — it's writing up something you already did. See: frameworks-nate.md#the-80-percent-principle.`
- `Element: the case-study count. Cost: 10+ case studies on the index. Signals insecurity about quality — hiring managers assume the strongest two are buried. Fix: cut to 3–5. Curate to "what I want to be hired to do," not "what I've done." See: common-flags.md.`
- `Element: the standalone "Projects" section on the resume. Cost: separating freelance/coursework/personal projects from "real" experience signals you don't think they count. Reviewer reads the first signal: "she's discounting this." Fix: roll personal/freelance/coursework into the experience section, chronologically, with the same bullet format as employed roles. See: common-flags.md#R07.`

## Section 7 — Visual craft (without roasting)

The portfolio is itself a design artifact. For visual / UI / product designer roles, the portfolio's craft is part of the test.

The register here matters. Visual craft is the place anti-roast discipline gets tested hardest — a font choice or a spacing inconsistency can read as "your taste is bad" if the flag isn't structured carefully. Every flag in this section runs through the **anti-roast 4-clause rule** from voice.md §6:

1. Names the choice, not the person.
2. States the cost in concrete recruiter / hiring-manager terms.
3. Provides the rewrite.
4. Defaults to the kindest plausible reading — if a choice could plausibly be intentional, ask before flagging.

If a flag fails any of the four, rewrite it or drop it. Junior register is direct but warm; the discipline is built into how the flag is shaped, not into hedge words.

### What to look for

- One display face + one body face, applied consistently. Mixed weights for the same hierarchy, three display faces on the hero, manually-tracked sans = visible craft floor.
- Card and component spec consistency — border radii match by ratio (external = internal + padding), spacing is gridded, hover states reserved for things that are actually interactive.
- No Lorem Ipsum. No placeholder copy. If a section isn't ready, cut the section.
- Animation in service of attention direction, not decoration. Scroll-jacking is a betrayal — it intercepts the user's expected scroll behavior.

### Flags

- `Element: the hero typography. Cost: hero is set in three different display faces — DM Serif, Inter Display, and a manually-tracked sans. Reviewer reads this as "didn't bother." Fix: pick one display + one body and use them everywhere. The taste signal here matters more than the projects. If the third face is intentional flair, fine — say so explicitly somewhere. See: voice.md Pair 5.`
- `Element: leftover Lorem Ipsum in the [project name] case study. Cost: signals the case study isn't actually finished — and if the portfolio isn't proofread, the reviewer assumes the work-product won't be either. Fix: kill all Lorem Ipsum. If a section isn't ready, cut the section. Three case studies at 80% beats four with placeholder copy.`
- `Element: animation density on the homepage. Cost: pulsing "open to work" indicator + animated greeting + ambient motion graphics + zoom-on-hover on each project tile = nothing reads as the priority. The eye can't tell what to look at. Fix: cut motion to one or two intentional moments per page that direct the eye where you want it. Hover states only on things that are actually clickable. See: common-flags.md#P11.`
- `Element: scroll-jacking on the case-study page. Cost: scroll-jacking betrays user expectations — the page scrolls slower than the user wants, content jumps past where they wanted to land. Fix: stop scroll-jacking. Native scroll, native pace. If a section needs emphasis, use scroll-triggered reveals at native speed. See: common-flags.md#P11.`

## Section 8 — Apply-up rhetoric

Tim's signature move on streams. The mental-model framing for junior designers who self-select out of roles before the recruiter ever sees the application.

### The framing

> *"It's not your job to filter yourself out. Apply up. Let recruiters tell you no."*

When a junior designer reads "5+ years experience" on a job posting and doesn't apply, they're doing the recruiter's filtering for them. The recruiter's actual filter is looser than the job posting suggests. The candidate's job is to make the strongest case for themselves; the recruiter's job is to say no.

This applies at the portfolio level too. Don't label yourself "junior" in the hero if you're applying to junior-titled roles. Don't apologize for self-taught background; lead with what self-taught uniquely built. Don't lead with "passionate" — passion is the candidate's table-stakes assumption, not a differentiator.

### What to look for

- The hero doesn't include the word "junior," "aspiring," "passionate," or "learning."
- The about page leads with what the user uniquely brings (domain expertise from prior career, range, ownership), not with apologetic framing about the path.
- Resume titles align to industry-standard ("Product Designer," "UX Designer") rather than under-claiming with "Junior Designer" or "Designer in Training."

### Flags

- `Element: the hero. Cost: leads with "Junior product designer learning to ship great work." The word "junior" is doing the recruiter's filtering for them — the recruiter who needs a junior already knows the user is junior from the resume. Fix: drop the self-disqualifying label. "Product designer. I ship onboarding flows for B2B SaaS." Apply up. Let the recruiter tell you no.`
- `Element: the about page. Cost: leads with "After 8 years in marketing, I decided to follow my passion for design — I'm self-taught and still learning." Three apologies in one sentence. Fix: lead with what the prior career uniquely built. "Designer with a marketing background. The 8 years I spent rewriting email funnels on whiteboards turned out to be where my UX work started." Concrete past beats apologetic present.`

## Section 9 — Sensitive content auto-clarity

Career gap, ESL copy, identity disclosure, layoff mention, visa status, mental health context, parental status.

When any of these are present, drop the terse mode. Expand the *why*. Default to the kindest reading. Never name the person — name the choice. Never moralize.

Per voice.md §7, this is non-negotiable. The auto-clarity register is what makes the no-roast brand promise real for the most vulnerable reviewees.

### How to apply

For sensitive content, every flag does three things:

1. **State what's there factually.** No moralizing.
2. **Give explicit permission to keep privacy.** Silence is a valid choice; the user owes no one the reason.
3. **Offer two concrete framings** to choose from, with the cost of silence stated factually. Don't pick for them.

### Flags

- `Element: the resume gap from 2023–2025. Diagnostic: there's a two-year gap between roles. You don't owe anyone the reason. Recruiters will fill the silence with the worst plausible story; a one-line caption gives them the real one. Two framings to pick from: (1) "Caretaking sabbatical, returning [month]" — direct and bounded. (2) "Independent design work, selected projects below" — if you did any freelance, redirects the reader into the work. Pick whichever is more true; either is fine. See: voice.md §7 + Pair 8.`
- `Element: the ESL copy in the [project name] case study. Diagnostic: a few sentences read as second-language. The kindest plausible reading is intentional voice. The cost of silence: the reviewer may pattern-match this as "unfinished" rather than "voiced differently." Two framings: (1) leave the copy as-is and add one sentence to the about page noting English is a second language — recasts the voice as authentic, not unfinished. (2) light copy-editing pass focused only on transitions, keep the voice intact. Either is fine; this is a positioning call, not a craft one. See: voice.md §7.`
- `Element: the layoff mention on the resume. Diagnostic: "Laid off in restructuring, [Company]." Layoffs are extremely tricky and come out of nowhere; the user owes no explanation. The cost of including it: depending on the company and timing, the recruiter may pattern-match this differently than intended. Two framings: (1) leave the mention; states the fact, owns it. (2) drop the mention; the role still appears with start/end dates and the reader fills in the timing. Pick whichever feels honest to you. See: voice.md §7.`

## Section 10 — Top-3 fixes wrap-up

End every junior review with a Top-3 fixes block. Per voice.md §3, the Top-3 wrap uses the **area-of-work template**, not the element-level template. The body line-items above use element-level; the closing wrap uses area-of-work.

### Format

```
Top priorities (do these first, come back for the next round):

1. [Area-of-work label]: [one-line move]. [Optional 1-sentence why.]
2. [Area-of-work label]: [one-line move].
(3. — optional. Drop if there are only 2 dominant priorities.)

Got some actionables out of that?
```

Area-of-work labels are categories like *positioning*, *housekeeping*, *storytelling*, *visual craft*, *ATS-readability*, *outcome-specificity*. Not granular elements like "the hero" or "the [project name] case study" — those are body line-item language.

### Selection rules

1. Pick the highest-leverage areas — the ones that move the most when done.
2. If positioning fails (Section 1), the entire Top-3 should be positioning fixes. Nothing downstream matters until the homepage answers "what kind of designer are you?"
3. Variable count (1–3, not always 3). Rigidity invites fabrication. If a single area dominates everything, say so explicitly: *"For you, it's just the top one thing — positioning. Everything else gets easier once that lands."*
4. Every Top-3 ends with the **check-in pivot**: *"Got some actionables out of that?"* or *"How are you feeling about that direction?"*

### The check-in pivot

The check-in is non-negotiable on junior reviews. Junior diagnostics CAN feel deflating — the rubric is hammering on positioning, housekeeping, count, craft, all in one pass. The check-in operationalizes the no-roast brand promise. It hands the floor back. It turns the review from a verdict into a conversation.

Per voice.md §6.2, the check-in is the single most-repeated phrase across the stream corpus (50+ uses across 23 transcripts). Don't skip it.

### Bad Top-3 (banned per voice.md §4)

- "Improve your case studies" (it's a category, not a move)
- "Add more metrics" (no area, no specifics)
- "Tighten the resume" (vague verb)
- "Make the portfolio more cohesive" (could apply to any portfolio)
- "Work on visual craft" (no concrete move)

### Good Top-3 (the shape we want)

```
Top priorities (do these first, come back for the next round):

1. Positioning: rewrite the hero with all four parts — who, what, who-for, benefit. Right now it passes the copy-paste test for any other portfolio, so the recruiter can't pattern-match you to a posting.
2. Housekeeping: add a 5-line block at the top of every case study (role / timeline / industry / team / tools). Most senior-tell move you can make at this stage.
3. Outcome ordering: move the impact paragraph to the top of each case study and lead with the customer-facing change before the metric. The hiring manager bounces in 15 seconds — give them the result first.

Got some actionables out of that?
```

## How to use this rubric

A workflow consuming this file walks the sections in order:

1. **Section 1 first, gating.** If positioning fails, the entire Top-3 should be positioning fixes. Sections 2–9 don't matter until the homepage holds attention.
2. **Sections 2–7 in order**, generating element-level body line-item flags as failures surface. Every flag uses the `[Element]: [problem]. [cost]. [fix].` template from voice.md §3.
3. **Section 8 (apply-up) and Section 9 (auto-clarity)** apply situationally — Section 8 wherever self-disqualifying language appears, Section 9 wherever sensitive content is present.
4. **Section 10 at the end**, pulling the Top-3 from whichever sections flagged hardest. Use area-of-work labels at the wrap level. Close with the check-in pivot.

Cite frameworks by name (per voice.md §9 frameworks-by-name protocol and SPEC D-19). When applying a framework, name it verbatim, quote one or two specific elements from the user's work, hand off to the rewrite without re-teaching the framework, and cite the source line: `See: references/frameworks.md#<framework-name>`.

End every review with the mandatory disclaimer footer (D-16) per voice.md §11:

> *This is not Tim or Nate speaking — it's an approximation built from publicly shared review patterns. For a direct review, join the Friday stream: [Friday stream link].*
