# Rubric — Senior portfolio + resume review

> Diagnostic. Read the senior portfolio for thesis, trajectory, and target environment. Don't prescribe.
> Source: 23 @designshaped Friday-stream transcripts. Joint Tim+Nate voice.

---

## Who this is for

Use this rubric when the user is:

- Mid-level designer building a portfolio for a senior application.
- Senior designer prepping promotion materials (often NDA-bound).
- Manager returning to IC who needs to re-establish ship-shaped evidence.
- IC senior at a company that has staff/principal levels above.

For first-job applicants, bootcamp grads, career switchers, and anyone in their first or second design role: use `rubric-junior.md`. The mode-switch in `SKILL.md` decides which rubric loads. They never load together.

---

## How senior review differs from junior review

Junior portfolios fail in repeating, prescriptive ways — the rubric reads "every junior portfolio needs a value prop in the hero." Senior portfolios fail in individual ways. Apply a checklist to a senior portfolio and the user gets generic feedback, which is worse than no feedback.

The senior tell is in the seams — **scope language, the bet, the through-line, target environment**. The work itself is usually competent. The diagnostic asks whether the seams add up to a coherent senior thesis or a generalist drift.

The flow:

1. **Extract the thesis** — what kind of senior is this person aiming at?
2. **Identify the target environment** — Centralized vs Decentralized.
3. **Read scope language in housekeeping** — the senior tell.
4. **Find the bet at the top of case studies** — execution-only fails this step.
5. **Validate the validation step** — the Problem → Process → Validation chain.
6. **Trajectory and coherence gaps** — does the body of work argue what the about page argues?
7. **Surface the probing questions** — what would a senior interviewer ask?
8. **Hard metrics vs soft metrics calibration** — behavior change as senior evidence.
9. **Anti-roast register at senior** — direct, with recalibration in play.
10. **Top-3 wrap-up** — area-of-work labels, variable count.

Each step below covers what to read for, what good looks like, and the framework to cite. Frameworks named verbatim, per D-19. The user can read the framework definition in `frameworks.md`; the rubric just names it and applies it.

## Step 1 — Extract the thesis

Before applying any criteria, read the body of work and answer: **"What kind of senior designer is this person trying to be?"**

The thesis is rarely stated. It's hidden in:

- **The about page.** What gets named first — domain, scope, posture, role evolution? What's missing?
- **The case-study openers.** Do they open at the bet ("we had a 40% drop-off and no one knew why"), at execution ("I was assigned the checkout flow"), or at process ("we ran a research sprint")? Senior thesis lives at the bet. Mid thesis lives at execution. Junior thesis lives at process.
- **Project selection.** Six projects on the portfolio — what's the through-line? "I ship 0-to-1 in fintech" is a thesis. "I scale design systems across N teams" is a thesis. "Here are six unrelated projects" is the absence of a thesis, which is itself the finding.
- **Role progression on the resume.** Did the user climb at one company (depth thesis) or breadth across many (range thesis)? Did they promote into management and back to IC (manager-returning thesis)?
- **What's NOT shown.** A research-heavy portfolio with no shipped product is a research-deep IC thesis. A systems-heavy portfolio with no flow work is a design-systems-lead thesis.

The thesis is the user's **shape**, not a role title. Read it through **Designer Niche Scales** (`frameworks-stream.md#niche-scales`) — niche is multi-axis, not one-dimensional. The four scales:

- **Domain familiarity** — agnostic ↔ embedded in one industry.
- **Elevation** — tactical ↔ strategic (UI output ↔ team leadership).
- **Product stage** — zero-to-one ↔ scaling enterprise.
- **Organization stage** — startup ↔ enterprise.

A senior thesis is one sentence that names the *posture* across these scales, not the *role title*. "I'm the strategic-elevation, fintech-embedded, scaling-stage IC at series B-D" is a thesis. "I'm a senior product designer" is not.

### When the thesis is unclear

If the workflow can't extract a thesis after reading the about page and 2-3 case-study openers, the FIRST flag is:

> Your portfolio doesn't tell us what you want to be. Six projects, all execution, no posture. Pick a thesis — name your shape across **Designer Niche Scales** — and rebuild the hero, the about page, and at least one case-study opener around it. Right now this reads as: "I'll take any senior role." Recruiters fill that silence with the worst plausible story.

This is the most common senior failure. We see it more than weak case studies, weak metrics, or weak craft. **No thesis is the problem under most other senior portfolio problems.**

## Step 2 — Identify the target environment

The load-bearing senior question. Apply **Centralized vs Decentralized Environments** (`frameworks-stream.md#centralized-decentralized`).

- **Centralized environment** — designer reports up to a UX manager who assigns a backlog. Common in agencies and smaller orgs. Prefers specialists.
- **Decentralized environment** — designer is embedded on a product team with PMs/devs/business stakeholders. Common in enterprise agile. Prefers generalists.

Stream rule: *"centralized environments prefer specialists. Decentralized environments prefer generalists."* Hybrid environments exist; some senior designers hold hybrid roles deliberately.

The senior diagnostic is not "is this person senior?" — most candidates targeting senior have the years. The diagnostic is: **does the portfolio reveal which environment they thrive in?**

What to read for:

- Case-study cadence language. Dual Track Agile rhythm, slicing, sprint-shaped delivery → decentralized signal. Project-shaped delivery, peer-design review, craft-mentorship language → centralized signal.
- Team composition in housekeeping. "1 PM, 3 engineers, 1 researcher" → decentralized embedded. "Team of 4 designers, 2 design leads" → centralized.
- About-page framing. "I broaden across product + ops + research" → decentralized generalist. "I deepen on craft and mentor a pod" → centralized specialist.

**The senior tell:** optimization for one environment is positioning. The lack of either signal is generalist drift. If a portfolio reads as both-or-neither, surface the mismatch:

> Your case studies argue for a decentralized environment — embedded on product teams, owning end-to-end delivery. Your about page positions for a design-craft-mentor role, which is centralized. Pick one. They optimize for different things, and recruiters in each environment read the cues differently.

This is the prerequisite question before applying any cadence framework. Use it before you reach for Slicing, Dual Track Agile, or House Analogy.

## Step 3 — Read scope language in housekeeping

The senior tell. Apply **Housekeeping** (`frameworks-stream.md#housekeeping`).

At junior, housekeeping shows "I was the designer." The role line reads as title-only.

At senior, housekeeping shows scope. The role line names team composition, surface ownership, reporting structure, time horizon, scale.

What good looks like:

- **Junior housekeeping (what we want at junior):** "Sole UX designer, B2B SaaS, Q3 2024, 4 months, Figma + Maze." Title, industry, time, tools.
- **Senior housekeeping (what we want at senior):** "Led design for a 12-engineer 2-PM team migrating 4M users from v1 to v2 over 7 months. I owned the IA + interaction design across 3 product surfaces; reported to the Director of Design; partnered with 2 staff engineers on the API contract." Specific scope, specific scale, named partnerships, surfaces.

What to surface when the housekeeping is junior-shaped on a senior portfolio:

> Housekeeping reads "Senior Product Designer, B2B SaaS, 2024." That's a junior-shape entry. For senior, housekeeping carries the scope — team size, partner roles, surface ownership, time horizon, scale. Your case study is missing the senior signal in the seam where it belongs. Rewrite: "Led design for a 12-engineer pod across 2 squads, owned IA + flows for the [surface], 7-month migration of 4M users."

The scope-signaling without humble-bragging rule: specific numbers don't read as bragging — they read as scope evidence. Vague superlatives ("high-impact," "transformative," "cross-functional") read as filler. The fix is always: replace the superlative with a number.

Senior housekeeping is also where **KSAs** (`frameworks-stream.md#ksas`) get seeded — extract knowledge, skills, abilities verbatim from the target job description and surface them in the housekeeping block. Senior portfolios often skip this because the user assumes the recruiter will read deeply. They don't.

## Step 4 — Find the bet at the top of case studies

The senior diagnostic question is: **"What was the bet, who decided, why this work and not other work?"**

Senior portfolios that read as execution fail this step. From streams (P14): senior is a level of decision-making, not pixel quality. If the case study can't show the user's thinking at the bet level — what was unclear, who decided to invest in it, why this work and not other work — the user is positioned at mid, not senior, regardless of years of experience.

Apply **Hook → Proof → Story** (`frameworks-stream.md#hook-proof-story`) with the senior shape:

- **Hook** — the bet. The business question. What was unclear, who decided to invest, why this and not other work.
- **Proof** — scope evidence. Visual or empirical. The shipped artifact + the scale claim.
- **Story** — stakeholder navigation, scope decisions, what was cut and why, cross-functional leadership moments.

What to read for:

- **Case-study openers.** Do they open with "the team decided to invest in checkout because retention was hemorrhaging at month 2" — or with "I was assigned the checkout flow"? The first is senior; the second is mid.
- **Decision evidence.** Is there a paragraph naming what was cut and why? Senior cases show what was *not* built. Mid cases show only what was built.
- **The "why this" question.** Six case studies on a senior portfolio — does each opener answer "why this work, not other work I could have shown"? If they don't, the portfolio reads as a project log, not a strategic argument.

When the bet is missing:

> Your case studies open at execution — *"I was assigned…"* / *"the PM gave me…"* / *"we needed to redesign…"*. For senior, they should open at the bet. What was unclear, who decided to invest in it, why this was the strategic move. Right now the case studies argue for a mid promotion, not a senior one. Move the bet to the top of at least your two strongest case studies.

The output gets smaller at senior; the decision trail gets bigger. At staff level, sometimes the case study doesn't even need final screens.

## Step 5 — Validate the validation step

Apply **CHAIN-01 (Problem → Process → Validation)** from the mock-interview probes. The validation step is the senior signal.

The streams call it out explicitly: *"that third question, how did you validate, is typically only found in mid-level candidates and above."*

Three questions, in order:

1. **What was the problem you were trying to solve, and why did it matter to the company?**
2. **How did you go about solving that problem? What was your process — how did you break the business problem into smaller pieces and negotiate stakeholders along the way?**
3. **How did you validate / measure success that the problem was in fact solved?**

Walk every case study against this chain. The presence or absence of a real validation step is the senior signal. What "real validation" looks like at senior:

- Hard metric with sourcing — "drop-off fell from 38% → 22%, n=14k, Mixpanel Q3 2024."
- Soft metric (behavior change) with specificity — "analysts no longer have to file tickets to find what they're looking for; this is now self-service."
- Honest non-validation — "we shipped to internal users only, no instrumentation; here's what I'd measure next time and why."

The senior failure mode here is performing validation without doing it: vague claims of success, "users loved it," metrics with no n. When the validation is performative:

> Case study claims *"40% drop-off reduction."* What's the n? Test cohort or live ship? By what scale did it actually move the business — was it your design or a bigger change? Right now this reads as a marketing-flavored claim, not a validated outcome. At senior, recruiters expect the n and the cohort and the attribution. Either add them or downshift to a behavior-change claim — *"the team adopted this as the default pattern across three surfaces"* — that you can defend.

This is also where **Q-05 (metric specificity)**, **Q-07 (trade-offs)**, and **Q-08 (stakeholder pushback)** get seeded for the prep handoff to Step 7.

## Step 6 — Trajectory and coherence gaps

This is where most senior portfolios actually fail. Not "missing axis Y" — *coherence*. The user states one thesis and the work argues another.

Common patterns:

**Pattern A — Thesis says systems, work says product.**

> Your stated thesis is design-systems lead — your about page names tokens, governance, adoption. But four of your six case studies are 0-to-1 product flows. Either re-thesis around the product work ("0-to-1 product designer who's also done systems work"), or replace two case studies with systems-shaped work — adoption rollout, deprecation case study, RFC-driven token migration. Right now the hero promises one thing and the body delivers another.

**Pattern B — Thesis says senior, work says mid.**

> Your case studies open at execution — "I was assigned…" / "the PM gave me…" / "we needed to redesign…". For your thesis (senior IC), they should open at the bet. Right now the case studies argue for a mid promotion, not a senior one.

**Pattern C — Thesis says strategic, work shows process.**

> The about page names strategic impact and cross-org influence. The case studies are heavy on research methodology and design iteration steps. For your thesis, the strategic context — what the company was betting on, what got de-prioritized, what you decided NOT to build — should be the loudest layer. Process is the second layer.

**Pattern D — Thesis says NDA-bound, no signal anywhere.**

> Your thesis (senior at a company you can't show work from) is real and common. But the portfolio shows two pre-current-job case studies and zero current-job signal. Even NDA-bound work can show: scope ("led design for a 12-engineer team across two product surfaces"), system ("owned the design-review process for 8 designers"), outcome at the org level ("reduced eng-design back-and-forth by 40%, measured via PR-revision count"). Right now the portfolio is silent on the most recent two years.

**Pattern E — Thesis says one environment, work shows the other.**

> About page positions for a centralized-environment role — peer mentorship, design-craft, design-org structure. Your case studies show decentralized embedded ownership — PM partnership, sprint cadence, end-to-end delivery. The two environments hire on different signals. Pick the one your strongest work supports and re-frame the about page.

When you see a coherence gap, name it directly:

> Your stated thesis is X. Your strongest evidence is Y. Either re-thesis around Y, or replace 2 case studies with X-shaped work.

Most users haven't asked themselves "does the work argue what the about page argues?"

## Step 7 — Surface the probing questions

What would a senior interviewer actually ask if this portfolio landed in their inbox? The senior probe set leans on simulation — "if I were the hiring manager…" — and on quoting the user's actual claims back at them.

Apply the canonical chain:

- **CHAIN-01 (Problem → Process → Validation)** for every case study.
- **CHAIN-02 (Metric → n → cohort → attribution)** when a number is cited.
- **Q-07 (trade-off probe)** — *"Strategy is just trade-offs. Walk us through the trade-off in [verbatim case study]. What did you give up, and would you give it up again?"*
- **Q-08 (stakeholder pushback)** — *"[Engineering / the PM / a stakeholder] pushed back. What did you concede? How did you negotiate that, and what does the case study not show that the negotiation cost you?"*
- **Q-13 (seniority-thesis check)** — *"Where in your career are you? Your hero reads as [archetype A]; your strongest case study shows [archetype B]. Which one are you?"*
- **Q-09 (craft-choice probe)** — *"Why did you choose [verbatim element]? What was the alternative you didn't take?"*
- **Q-14 (shipped-vs-conceptual)** — when conceptual work mixes with shipped work without distinction.

Each probe quotes the user's actual work. Generic "tell me about your design process" probes are forbidden — they don't test anything. The senior probe shape is always:

> [Quote a specific element from the user's work] → [diagnostic question that tests judgment, not method].

When generating probes for the user's interview prep, name three specific to their portfolio. Not three generic ones.

## Step 8 — Hard metrics vs soft metrics calibration

Apply **Hard Metrics vs Soft Metrics** (`frameworks-stream.md#hard-soft-metrics`).

The senior calibration: behavior change is real evidence when hard metrics aren't available. Drop the "must have metrics" prescriptive at senior. Senior portfolios are often NDA-bound, side-project-driven, or organizationally-instrumented in ways the designer didn't control.

The three escalating moves for the no-metric problem:

1. **Hard metric** (best when available) — quantifiable business or product number with sourcing. *"Drop-off fell 38% → 22%, n=14k, Mixpanel Q3 2024."*
2. **Behavior change / soft metric** (good when hard isn't accessible) — qualitative validated outcome. *"Analysts no longer have to use this software to find what they're looking for."* / *"The team adopted this pattern across 3 product surfaces."* Specific, validated, defensible.
3. **Prototyping with Numbers** (`frameworks-stream.md#prototyping-with-numbers`, creative when neither is available) — pick a comparable real product, find its public business numbers, scale the prediction down to your project. *"If there was an app similar to mine, here's what the business outcome would have been at this scale."*

The senior failure mode is fabricated round percentages — *"increased engagement by 45%"* on uninstrumented work. Hiring managers identify these on sight. Mature framing beats fake numbers every time:

> Case study claims *"engagement +45%."* On uninstrumented student work, that reads as fabricated. Three honest options: (1) replace with a behavior-change claim — what specific thing changed about how users interacted with the surface? (2) Replace with prototyping with numbers — name a comparable product, cite its public metric, predict yours at scale. (3) Cut the metric and describe what you'd have measured and why you couldn't. All three beat the round percentage.

For NDA-bound senior work, the move is housekeeping at the right level of abstraction (Step 3) plus the soft-metric language. Process and decision-making are almost never bound by NDAs.

## Step 9 — Anti-roast register at senior

The senior register is direct, but recalibration moves are more in play than at junior. Why: senior reviewees push back more readily. They've been doing the work for years. They know when the reviewer is wrong.

The 4-clause anti-roast rule (`voice.md` §6) still holds at senior. The bluntness ceiling is higher — senior reviewees have earned more direct framing — but bluntness ≠ roasting. Bluntness names the choice and the cost; roasting names the person.

Recalibration moves to keep in play at senior:

- **Permission-giving on severity.** *"That is real nitpicky though. If you only have time for three things, this isn't one of them."*
- **Naming the limit of expertise.** *"Our primary lens is product and UX — for visual-craft-only judgments, take this with a grain of salt."*
- **Verdict-word walk-back.** *"The hero positioning is wrong here — actually, I shouldn't say wrong. Wrong is subjective. The hero reads as agnostic when your strongest evidence is fintech-specific. That's a positioning mismatch, not a wrong call."*
- **Kindest plausible reading default.** *"If you've intentionally led with the systems work because the systems thesis is what you want to sell, totally fine. If the ordering was incidental, here's the move."*

If a Top-3 item would land as a roast, it's wrong, not the rule.

The **Tim says / Nate says protocol** (`voice.md` §8) lands more often at senior than junior. Senior reviews are where the rare divergences surface — Tim sometimes argues to keep a project Nate would cut, or vice versa. Per voice.md, callouts are added MANUALLY by Tim and Nate; the model never fabricates divergence. If you don't know they'd split, don't pretend they would.

## Step 10 — Top-3 wrap-up at senior

Per `voice.md` §3, the closing wrap uses the **area-of-work template** (not the body line-item template):

```
Top priorities (do these first, come back for the next round):

1. [Area-of-work label]: [one-line move]. [Optional 1-sentence why.]
2. [Area-of-work label]: [one-line move].
(3. — optional. Drop if there are only 2 dominant priorities.)

Got some actionables out of that?
```

Area-of-work labels at senior level: *positioning*, *housekeeping*, *the bet*, *validation*, *coherence*, *scope language*, *target environment*, *NDA framing*. Variable count (1-3, not always 3 — rigidity invites fabrication). Build in the defer-to-next-session clause where applicable. Preserve the "top three" phrasing even when count is smaller — it's load-bearing voice.

Senior Top-3 priority order:

1. **Thesis or target environment** (if missing or incoherent). Always position #1 if it applies. Without thesis or environment match, the rest of the review is sand.
2. **One case study to replace, upgrade, or rewrite at the bet level.** Specific, named.
3. **One scope/housekeeping/validation upgrade pattern.** Specific bullet, quoted verbatim. The fix is the rewrite.

Senior reviews can be MORE direct in the Top-3 — the audience has earned bluntness. But "no roasting" still holds.

## Senior resume rubric

Senior resumes are simpler structurally than junior ones (fewer sections, less to prove) but harder to write. The bar is scope language, not outcome verbs.

### Apply What/How/Why ↔ Why/How/What

`frameworks-stream.md#what-how-why`. Walk every bullet. Does it lead with the why? If a bullet starts with "Designed X," "Created Y," or "Led Z," it's What-first — flip it. The headline rule from streams: *"if you follow a what-how-why approach, really hard to write a bad bullet."*

The senior optimization: lead with impact (Why), then method (How), then activity (What).

- **Bad (What-first):** "Designed a new onboarding flow."
- **Mid (Why-first, weak):** "Designed a new onboarding flow that reduced drop-off by 18%."
- **Senior (Why → How → What, full):** "Reduced onboarding drop-off from 38% → 22% by leading the redesign of auth + first-run for a $200M-ARR SaaS — partnered with 6 engineers across 2 squads, the new pattern was adopted across 3 product surfaces, and the engineering partnership produced a reusable Auth flow library."

The senior bullet shows: scope (you owned it), measurable change (drop-off), org-level adoption (other surfaces), and second-order outcome (a library that outlived the project).

### Scope verbs

Led, owned, shipped, scaled, drove, partnered, established, restructured, defined.

Avoid:
- "Designed" without modifier (every designer designed).
- "Worked on" / "Contributed to" (low-agency).
- "Responsible for" (passive, junior register).
- "Helped" (anywhere).

### Measurable scope (specific numbers, not superlatives)

- Team size led — "Led design for a 4-designer pod" / "Mentored 6 designers."
- Engineering partner ratio — "Partnered with 12 engineers across 3 squads."
- Customer base / scale — "4M MAU" / "$200M ARR" / "200-engineer org."
- Time horizon — "18-month migration" / "Q3 2024 launch through Q2 2025 stability."
- Cross-org reach — "Adopted by 8 product teams" / "Influenced 3 quarterly OKRs."

If a bullet has no number, ask whether it's load-bearing. Most senior bullets without numbers can be cut.

### Reverse-chronological forcing function

The most senior signal — biggest scope, broadest influence — should be at the top. If the user's most senior work is 3 years old and their last 18 months are weaker, the resume undermines itself.

Fix: either lead with the older work explicitly framed ("Most recent senior IC role; current role is a managerial pivot") or restructure the bullets within the recent role to surface the senior signal that's there.

### Length

- 5-10 years experience: 1-2 pages.
- 10+ years: 2 pages, max.
- One page is fine if the work is dense enough; two pages is fine if every bullet earns its line.

The one-page rule is a religion that originated when resumes were stapled. In a digital-portal era, the constraint is irrelevant. Senior who cuts to one page loses the chance to describe scope.

### Common senior resume flags

- **Manager-shaped bullets in an IC application** — "Hired 4 designers, ran performance reviews" reads as a manager applying for IC. Reframe or cut.
- **Tool list dominates the skills section** — Senior resumes don't sell on tools. Trim and add scope language.
- **Recent role is thin** — 18 months with 2 bullets reads as "I'm coasting." Either flesh out (find scope language for the work that was done) or address the thinness directly.
- **Title inflation visible in role progression** — Going from "Senior Designer" at a Series-A to "Designer II" at a Series-C reads as a downgrade. Frame the move ("joined for the staff trajectory" / "domain pivot").

## NDA work — promotion-track-specific

Senior portfolios are often promotion-track or recently-departed-from-employer. The strongest work is usually NDA-bound. Junior advice ("ship something public, even small") doesn't apply at this level — the user has shipped real things, just not publicly.

How to talk about NDA work without violating it:

- Name the **shape** of the work without naming the product. ("Led the redesign of a B2B sales-tooling flow at a Series-C SaaS company.")
- Name the **scope** without naming the surface. ("Owned IA, interaction design, and rollout across 3 product surfaces, partnering with 12 engineers and 2 PMs.")
- Name the **outcome** at the right level of abstraction. ("Reduced sales-cycle drop-off by 22% in the test cohort" — without naming the company, the product, or the specific feature.)
- Use **before/after framing without screens.** A flow diagram, a process diagram, or a structural sketch can carry the story without leaking the UI.
- When asked, **walk-through framing**: "the portfolio sets the stage; the interview conversation is where the depth lives."

First check: many users haven't re-read their actual NDA and assume restrictions that aren't there. Process and decision-making are almost never bound. Talk about how you broke down the problem, the negotiation moves, the trade-offs — using synthetic data for screens. Frame the limitations honestly: "Specific data and screens are protected, here's what I can show about the thinking." That's a stronger story than fake screens.

### The "promotion-track portfolio" problem

If the portfolio is for an internal promotion review, the audience is your manager and your skip-level — not a recruiter. The structure flips: name the company, the team, the metric. NDA-shaped framing isn't required. The risk inverts — too much abstraction reads as evasion. State the work plainly.

If the same portfolio has to do double duty (promotion review *and* future external job search), recommend two versions: one internal, one external.

## Diagnostic vs prescriptive — the key difference

Junior rubric (`rubric-junior.md`): *"every case study needs an outcome paragraph."*

This rubric: *"your case studies open at execution; for your thesis, they should open at the bet."*

The senior rubric never says "every senior should." It always says "given THIS senior's thesis is X, they need Y."

When in doubt, re-anchor on the user's thesis. If the workflow is producing senior-feedback that would apply to any senior, the workflow is doing junior-shaped feedback in senior clothing — back up and re-extract the thesis.

## How a workflow uses this rubric

`workflows/review-portfolio.md` and `workflows/review-resume.md` consume this file when the user has selected the senior career-stage branch. The workflow's first move is Step 1 (extract thesis) and Step 2 (target environment) before any flagging or rewriting. Steps 3-8 produce the body of the review. Step 9 calibrates register. Step 10 produces the priority section.

`common-flags.md` is consulted for repeating patterns the user might also have, but flags only fire if they're consistent with the user's thesis. The combined-review workflow runs both the resume and portfolio passes, then surfaces coherence gaps between resume bullets and case-study claims.

Frameworks named verbatim throughout, per D-19. The user can read the framework definition in `frameworks.md`; this rubric just names it and applies it.
