# Heuristics — Portfolio Rx

## How this file is used

Workflows cite these heuristics when they directly apply to a critique. Use them sparingly — over-citing degrades the voice. One or two heuristics per review at most.

These are smaller-grain than `common-flags.md`. Common flags are portfolio/resume-specific patterns; heuristics are general design rules of thumb that occasionally illuminate a specific portfolio problem. Cite by name (per D-19), don't paraphrase, and only when it adds a real second angle.

## Heuristics

### "outcomes and not outputs" {#outcomes-not-outputs}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "An Introduction To Slicing." https://nabauer.com/articles/slicing
**When it applies:** Case study lists deliverables (wireframes, mockups, prototypes, specs) and treats the artifact list as the result. Cite when a portfolio reads as a deliverable inventory and we want to push the user toward what changed for the user/business.
**Example flag:** The [project name] case study lists six deliverables and stops. Outcomes, not outputs — what changed for the user when this shipped? Add one paragraph: the metric, the user-facing change, or the business decision the work unlocked.

### "Why is that important?" {#why-is-that-important}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Capturing Brand Drivers." https://nabauer.com/articles/capturing-brand-drivers
**When it applies:** Case study claims an outcome ("users were happier," "the team adopted it") and stops at the surface benefit. Cite when we want the user to push one layer deeper into why the outcome mattered.
**Example flag:** Case study says "support tickets dropped." Why is that important? Cost saved, headcount freed, CSAT moved? Add the next layer — the metric is the surface, the consequence is the case.

### One to five drivers, never more {#one-to-five-drivers}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Capturing Brand Drivers." https://nabauer.com/articles/capturing-brand-drivers
**When it applies:** Portfolio hero or about page lists eight things the designer is good at. Resume summary names ten specialties. Cite when we want the user to narrow positioning.
**Example flag:** Hero claims six specialties — research, IA, UI, prototyping, design systems, accessibility. One to five, never more. Pick the two that get the user hired for this role and put the others below the fold.

### Don't combine across columns {#dont-combine-across-columns}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Capturing Brand Drivers." https://nabauer.com/articles/capturing-brand-drivers
**When it applies:** User has tried to merge functional-and-emotional benefits into one line ("makes accounting fast and joyful") and the result is muddy. Cite when we want them to keep dimensions separate.
**Example flag:** The summary line collapses functional and emotional into one phrase ("ships work that's both fast and delightful"). Don't combine across columns — pick the dimension that does the most work for this role and own it. Keep the other for a different sentence.

### Open-ended questions only {#open-ended-questions-only}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "When to Interview, Survey, and Focus Group." https://nabauer.com/articles/when-to-interview-survey-and-focus-group
**When it applies:** User's research write-up shows yes/no or rating-only questions and treats the result as user insight. Cite when the research section claims insight that the question shape couldn't produce.
**Example flag:** The research bullet says "we asked users if they liked the new flow — 8/10 said yes." Open-ended questions only — "8/10 said yes" tells us the user followed instructions, not what they think. Rewrite the question and the bullet: what did users say about the flow when we didn't pre-load the answer?

### Focus on the customer, not the product {#focus-on-the-customer}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "When to Interview, Survey, and Focus Group." https://nabauer.com/articles/when-to-interview-survey-and-focus-group
**When it applies:** Case study research section asks users about the product ("how would you change this button?") instead of about the user's job-to-be-done. Cite when the research questions show product-myopia.
**Example flag:** The research section asks users to evaluate the redesign. Focus on the customer, not the product — instead of "how would you change this button," ask "walk me through the last time you tried to do [the underlying task]." The product question caps the answer; the customer question opens it.

### "Don't call it a survey" {#dont-call-it-a-survey}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "When to Interview, Survey, and Focus Group." https://nabauer.com/articles/when-to-interview-survey-and-focus-group
**When it applies:** User's mock interview prep or case study describes "running a survey" as the research method. Cite when we want them to reframe the research as conversation.
**Example flag:** Case study says "I ran a survey of 12 users." Don't call it a survey — twelve people isn't a survey, it's twelve interviews. Reframe the bullet: "I ran 12 user interviews, one question at a time, and pattern-matched the responses." Same work, more credibly described.

### Skip focus groups {#skip-focus-groups}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "When to Interview, Survey, and Focus Group." https://nabauer.com/articles/when-to-interview-survey-and-focus-group
**When it applies:** Case study cites a focus group as the primary research method, especially for an interface-level decision. Cite when we want the user to defend the method choice or swap to interviews.
**Example flag:** Case study leads with "we ran a focus group." Skip focus groups — the loud voice in the room dominates and the quiet ones never speak. Either reframe what we did as paired interviews, or note the limitation: "We chose a focus group for budget reasons; in retrospect three 1:1 interviews would have given cleaner signal."

### Free coffee > paid incentives {#free-coffee}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "When to Interview, Survey, and Focus Group." https://nabauer.com/articles/when-to-interview-survey-and-focus-group
**When it applies:** Mock interview prep or research write-up describes paying users $50–$100 incentives and treats high incentive as the credibility signal. Cite when we want the user to reframe what makes research credible.
**Example flag:** Research section opens with "we paid $100 per user for 30-minute sessions." Free coffee > paid incentives — paid users perform research; coffee-bribed ones tell the truth. Note this trade-off in the case study, or describe the recruiting differently: "We recruited via [community/Slack/network] and the conversations were candid."

### Personas evolve {#personas-evolve}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Proto Personas." https://nabauer.com/articles/proto-personas
**When it applies:** Case study presents personas as a fixed deliverable from week 2 of the project that never changed. Cite when we want the user to show research literacy by acknowledging persona drift.
**Example flag:** Case study shows the persona slide and stops. Personas evolve — what did we learn six weeks in that we got wrong about Sarah-the-ops-manager? Add one line: how the persona changed as the project progressed. That's the senior signal.

### Get feedback at 80% {#feedback-at-80}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "UX, Agile, and Healthcare." https://nabauer.com/articles/ux-agile-and-healthcare-innovation-projects
**When it applies:** User's portfolio shows polished final UI with no mid-process feedback evidence. Or case study describes a 6-month silent build. Cite when we want them to show iterative learning.
**Example flag:** Case study jumps from research to launch with no mid-fidelity feedback loop visible. Get feedback at 80% — the last 20% costs as much as the first 80%. Add the rough mid-fi prototype, the user reactions to it, and what we changed before final. That's where the work lives.

### Stay one or two slices ahead {#one-or-two-slices-ahead}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "An Introduction To Slicing" + "UX, Agile, and Healthcare." https://nabauer.com/articles/slicing
**When it applies:** Case study shows the designer designed the entire product up-front, before any of it shipped. Cite when we want them to surface the dual-track-agile reality.
**Example flag:** Case study presents 14 fully-designed screens before describing what shipped. Stay one or two slices ahead — designing 14 screens up-front means most of that work either gets thrown away or doesn't match what the team builds. Reframe: which two slices were we ahead on at any given time, and what changed because dev feedback came back?

### Logos last, pathos first {#logos-last}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Three Principles Of Persuasion" + "What Aristotle Can Teach Us About Marketing." https://nabauer.com/articles/three-principles-of-persuasion
**When it applies:** Case study or portfolio hero opens with technical specs, framework choices, or process diagrams before establishing the human stake. Cite when the work leads with logos when it should lead with pathos.
**Example flag:** The [project name] case study opens with the technical architecture diagram. Logos last, pathos first — the reader doesn't care about the architecture until they care about the user problem. Move the user moment to the top; the architecture earns its place once the reader is invested.

### Sell the category before the product {#sell-the-category}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "What Aristotle Can Teach Us About Marketing" + "How Ladders Alter Your Marketing Strategies." https://nabauer.com/articles/what-aristotle-can-teach-us-about-marketing
**When it applies:** User is targeting an emerging or unfamiliar role/domain (AI design, dev tools, B2B fintech) and the portfolio assumes the reader already cares about the domain. Cite when we want them to make the category case before the work case.
**Example flag:** Hero positions the user as "AI product designer" and lists projects, but the portfolio assumes the reader already cares about AI design. Sell the category before the product — one paragraph on why AI product design is a distinct discipline (and what specifically the user brings to it) before showing the work. Otherwise the reader skims.

### Make a new ladder {#make-a-new-ladder}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "How Ladders Alter Your Marketing Strategies." https://nabauer.com/articles/how-ladders-alter-your-marketing-strategies
**When it applies:** Senior designer with strong but generalist work competing against deeper specialists for the same role. Or career switcher trying to outcompete bootcamp grads on bootcamp-grad terms. Cite when we want them to reframe positioning.
**Example flag:** Portfolio competes head-on with senior generalists from FAANG. Make a new ladder — the user can't out-FAANG FAANG; they can be the top rung on a different ladder ("senior designer who ships in regulated B2B from a research-deep background"). Rewrite the hero around the ladder where the user is rung 1.

### The UX designer is the Agile facilitator {#ux-designer-is-facilitator}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "UX, Agile, and Healthcare." https://nabauer.com/articles/ux-agile-and-healthcare-innovation-projects
**When it applies:** Senior portfolio missing the facilitation/leadership signal. Case studies show execution but not how the designer kept the team aligned, unblocked, or moving. Cite when we want them to surface invisible leadership work.
**Example flag:** Senior case study shows execution and outcomes but no team-alignment work. The UX designer is the Agile facilitator — at this level, the work isn't just the design; it's keeping six people moving in one direction. Add one paragraph: how we ran the standups, what we did when eng pushed back, what we said in the retro. That's the senior signal.

### Quantity over quality in ideation {#quantity-over-quality}
**Source:** Nate Bauer, "Capturing Brand Drivers." https://nabauer.com/articles/capturing-brand-drivers
**When it applies:** Case study shows three sketches and one final solution and presents the path as obvious. Cite when we want the user to surface the bad ideas they killed.
**Example flag:** Case study shows three options and the chosen one. Quantity over quality in ideation — three options doesn't tell us we explored; thirty does. Either show the wider exploration ("we sketched 24 directions, here are the four we tested") or note the constraint ("scope forced an early narrow"). Bad ideas ruled out is part of the work.

## Adding new heuristics (procedure)

- New heuristics come from two sources: (1) Nate's published writing as it grows, and (2) Tim+Nate transcript mining where a phrase recurs across multiple streams. For (2), the mining-log.md (private, gitignored per D-17) tracks recurrence; three independent appearances minimum before promotion. Each new heuristic gets a verbatim quote header, a source citation, "When it applies," and one example flag that passes voice.md §9 density.
- Append only — never rewrite an existing heuristic in place. If the framing genuinely changes (e.g. Nate retracts or revises a published rule), add a new heuristic and mark the old one with a "superseded by" note rather than deleting it. The Index list at the top stays in append order so anchor links don't shift.
